# GNN-Based Patient Population Graph for Early-Onset Breast Cancer Stratification

This repository contains the implementation and comparative analysis of **Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)** versus traditional machine learning baselines for predicting the age of onset in breast cancer patients[cite: 1, 2]. By treating a patient cohort as a population-level graph—where nodes represent individuals and edges represent genotypic similarity—this project explores the utility of geometric deep learning in clinical oncology[cite: 3, 11].

---

## 📂 Project Overview

Predicting early-onset breast cancer is challenging due to the complex interaction between high-penetrance rare variants and low-effect common variants[cite: 8]. Our research investigates whether **Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN)** and **Graph Attention Networks (GAT)** can capture non-linear patterns that traditional linear models or Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS) might miss[cite: 3, 10].

Despite the theoretical promise of "guilt-by-association" learning, our findings indicate that GNNs currently struggle with the high noise-to-signal ratio and extreme class imbalance inherent in genomic datasets[cite: 4, 6].

---

## 🛠️ Repository Structure

The codebase is organized to support a complete pipeline from data simulation to model evaluation:

* **`model_train.ipynb`**: The primary entry point. This notebook orchestrates the entire processing workflow, including data loading, graph construction, and model execution.
* **`graph_nn.py`**: Core logic for the GNN architectures. It handles the construction of the $k$-NN graph structure, training loops, and evaluation metrics for GCN and GAT[cite: 17, 36].
* **`simu.py`**: A utility for data simulation. It is specifically designed to address the 1:6.5 early-onset to late-onset ratio by generating balanced synthetic samples or managing stratified sampling[cite: 20].
* **`xgb.py` & `xgboost_baseline.ipynb`**: Implementation of the XGBoost baseline. These files demonstrate that non-linear tree-based models often outperform graph models in low-sample regimes[cite: 14, 48].
* **`mlp.py`**: Implementation of a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) to serve as a non-graph deep learning baseline.
* **`plot.py`**: Visualization scripts used to generate performance curves and data distribution plots found in the `/pics` directory.

## 🔬 Methodology & Model Analysis

The project implements the graph convolution operation defined as:
$$H^{(l+1)} = \sigma\left( \tilde{D}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \tilde{A} \tilde{D}^{-\frac{1}{2}} H^{(l)} W^{(l)} \right)$$ [cite: 36]

### Key Challenges Identified:
* **Over-smoothing**: GNNs tend to average the representations of early-onset patients with their late-onset neighbors due to the lack of clear spatial clustering in the SNP space[cite: 27, 63].
* **Low Homophily**: t-SNE projections show significant intermingling of classes, meaning edges in a $k$-NN graph often connect nodes with different labels[cite: 22, 26].
* **Sample Scale**: With only 74 early-onset cases in the TCGA-BRCA cohort, deep architectures like GNNs are prone to overfitting compared to regularized linear models[cite: 41, 69].

## Results Summary

The refined methodology highlights a "learning plateau" for graph models:
- **XGBoost**: AUROC 0.674 [cite: 48, 49]
- **GAT**: AUROC 0.595 [cite: 49]
- **GCN**: AUROC 0.547 [cite: 49]

While GAT shows marginal promise through its attention mechanism—which prioritizes informative neighbors—traditional models remain more robust for small-scale genomic stratification tasks.
